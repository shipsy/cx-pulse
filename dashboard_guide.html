1 Filters Applied
Every metric in this dashboard uses the same base filter. This ensures consistency — no metric uses a different scope.

Base Filter Rules

These four rules are applied to every query before any metric is computed.

Subtype = "Support" (exact match)
Customer Cohort NOT IN ("WMS", "Roadmap")
POD NOT IN ("WMS Inbound", "WMS Outbound")
Stage != "canceled"
Why we filter: Without this, the numbers would include ~2000+ non-support tickets (projects, UAT, internal items, tech_debt) that dilute the support metrics. WMS has a separate team and its own tracking. Roadmap items are feature requests, not support tickets.
Why does DevRev show 7768 tickets but your dashboard shows 1065?
DevRev has all ticket types (Support, Project, UAT, tech_debt, etc.). We filter to Support-subtype only (1833), then exclude WMS/Roadmap scope (768 tickets) = 1065 support tickets in our scope. The difference is not missing data — it is intentional scoping.
Why exclude canceled tickets?
Canceled tickets were never real support work. They include auto-generated spam, duplicates, and misrouted items. Counting them would inflate both "created" and "resolved" numbers without reflecting any actual work done.
What if a ticket has no cohort set?
Tickets with cohort = "TBD" or empty are still included in the dashboard (they pass the filter). They show up as "TBD" or "Unassigned" in per-account breakdowns. These need manual triage to be assigned a proper cohort.

2 KPI Cards
The top-level numbers shown as cards at the top of the dashboard. These give the 10-second summary of support health.

Created

Number of tickets created in the selected date range.

Count tickets WHERE
Created date IN (From, To)
AND stage != canceled

Resolved

Number of tickets resolved or closed in the selected date range.

Count tickets WHERE
Close date IN (From, To)
AND stage IN (resolved, Closed)
AND stage != canceled

Resolve Ratio

Are we keeping up with incoming volume? >100% means queue is shrinking, <100% means queue is growing.

Resolve Ratio = Resolved / Created x 100

FR% (First Response Hit Rate)

Percentage of tickets where first response was within SLA target time.

FR% = count(Metric Status[0] = "hit")
/ count(Metric Status[0] IN ("hit", "miss"))
x 100

RT% (Resolution Time Hit Rate)

Percentage of tickets resolved within SLA target time.

RT% = count(Metric Status[1] = "hit")
/ count(Metric Status[1] IN ("hit", "miss"))
x 100

Resolution P50

Median resolution time. 50% of tickets are resolved faster than this value.

PERCENTILE(Completed In[1], 0.50)
WHERE Completed In[1] > 0
Critical distinction — SLA-aware time vs wall-clock time: The P50 value uses DevRev's Completed In[1] field, which is SLA-aware. It excludes time in "Awaiting Customer Response" (SLA paused), excludes non-business hours (nights, weekends per Mon-Fri 10AM-8PM IST schedule), and only counts actual support work time. This is NOT Close date - Created date.

Open Tickets

Count of tickets NOT in a terminal stage.

Count WHERE stage NOT IN
(resolved, canceled, Closed)

Blockers

Open tickets with Severity = "Blocker" — customer's core business operation is completely stopped.

Count WHERE stage NOT IN
(resolved, canceled, Closed)
AND severity = "Blocker"

CSAT

Customer Satisfaction Score — average rating from post-resolution surveys. Scale: 1-5 (5 = very satisfied).

Average of CSAT Rating[0].average
WHERE CSAT Rating[0].count > 0
Why not count canceled as resolved?
Canceled means the ticket was invalid — auto-generated spam, duplicates, misrouted. Counting it inflates both created and resolved without reflecting real work.
Resolve ratio is 85%. Is that good?
It means 85 out of every 100 tickets created were resolved in the same period. The queue grew by 15%. We need >100% consistently to shrink the backlog. Occasional dips below 100% during high-inflow weeks are normal, but sustained <100% indicates a capacity problem.
What's the SLA target for FR and RT?
The default SLA policy is "Support Ticket SLA Default" (sla-28), applied to all accounts. The schedule is Mon-Fri 10AM-8PM IST. Individual account targets may vary in the future if per-account SLA policies are configured in DevRev.
Why is RT% higher than FR%?
RT measures total resolution time, which can be paused during "Awaiting Customer Response." FR measures time to first response with no pausing allowed. FR is harder to meet because the clock starts immediately when the ticket is created.
FR% and RT% exclude "in_progress" — why?
Tickets with Metric Status = "in_progress" haven't completed SLA evaluation yet (they're still open). Including them would dilute the metric. We only count completed evaluations: either "hit" (met SLA) or "miss" (did not).
Why is P50 only 1.9 hours but customers complain about multi-day waits?
1.9h is the SLA-aware time — actual support work effort. The customer experiences wall-clock time which includes nights, weekends, and time they spend responding to our questions. A 3-day calendar wait might only be 1.9h of actual support effort. The customer perception is real, but P50 measures something different: how efficient the team is with their working time.
Only 50 CSAT responses out of 1000 resolved. Is that representative?
5% response rate is typical for email surveys in B2B SaaS. Respondents tend to be either very happy or very unhappy, so the score may be slightly polarized. However, it's the best signal we have. To increase response rate, we'd need in-app prompts or shorter surveys.

4 Resolution Time (TAT) SLA-AWARE
How long it takes to resolve tickets, measured in SLA-aware minutes. This is the most commonly misunderstood metric — read this section carefully.

Source Field

Completed In[1] from DevRev's SLA tracker. This field automatically:

Pauses when ticket is in "Awaiting Customer Response"
Pauses during non-business hours (Mon-Fri 10AM-8PM IST)
Only counts minutes where support was actively responsible

P10 (10th Percentile)

10% of tickets are resolved in this time or less. These are the fastest, simplest tickets.

PERCENTILE(Completed In[1], 0.10)
WHERE Completed In[1] > 0

P50 (Median)

50% faster, 50% slower. The "typical" resolution time. This is the headline number.

PERCENTILE(Completed In[1], 0.50)
WHERE Completed In[1] > 0

P90 (90th Percentile)

90% of tickets are resolved in this time or less. Only 10% take longer. This shows the "worst case" for most tickets.

PERCENTILE(Completed In[1], 0.90) WHERE Completed In[1] > 0
Why P10/P50/P90, not average? Average is skewed by outliers. One ticket taking 30 days pulls the average up massively. Percentiles give a clearer picture: P50 tells you the typical experience, P90 tells you the worst common experience.
Display format: <60 min shows as "Xm", <24h as "Xh", 24h+ as "Xd". Example: "1.9h" means 1 hour 54 minutes of SLA-aware work time.
Zero exclusion: Tickets with Completed In = 0 are excluded. These are tickets where SLA was paused the entire duration (e.g., immediately moved to "Awaiting Customer Response" and closed from there). Zero doesn't mean "instant" — it means "no SLA clock time was recorded."
P50 is 1.9h but customers complain about 3-day waits?
1.9h is support work time. The customer experiences wall-clock time which includes nights, weekends, and time waiting for their own response. A 3-day calendar wait might only be 1.9h of actual support effort. Both perspectives are valid — P50 measures team efficiency, while customer perception includes all waiting time.
Blocker P50 is 4.2 days. Why so slow for critical issues?
Blockers are the hardest problems — they often need engineering involvement, code changes, deployments, and cross-team coordination. The SLA-aware 4.2 days is actual work time across multiple people, not calendar time. These tickets typically have the most back-and-forth between support, engineering, and the customer.
Can we show wall-clock time too?
Yes — it would be Close date - Created date. But this mixes apples and oranges: a ticket created Friday 6 PM and resolved Monday 10 AM is "3 days" by wall clock but only a few hours of work. The SLA-aware metric is more fair for measuring team performance. Wall-clock time is better for measuring customer experience.

5 First Response Time SLA-AWARE
Time from ticket creation to first meaningful response. Uses the same SLA-aware measurement as Resolution Time.

Source Field

Completed In[0] from DevRev's SLA tracker. Same SLA-awareness as Resolution Time — excludes off-hours (Mon-Fri 10AM-8PM IST schedule).

P10 = PERCENTILE(Completed In[0], 0.10)
P50 = PERCENTILE(Completed In[0], 0.50)
P90 = PERCENTILE(Completed In[0], 0.90)
WHERE Completed In[0] > 0
Key difference from Resolution Time: First Response has no "pausing" concept — once a ticket is created, the FR clock ticks until someone responds. There is no stage that pauses the FR timer (unlike Resolution Time which pauses during Awaiting Customer Response).
FR P50 is 15 minutes. That seems fast?
Many tickets get a quick first response during business hours — either an auto-acknowledgment or a human doing initial triage. The P90 shows the tail: 10% of tickets wait much longer. Focus on P90 to understand the worst-case customer experience for first response.
Does the Friday AI agent count as a first response?
It depends on how the response is recorded in DevRev. If Friday posts an internal-only comment, it does not count as a customer-facing first response. If it posts a customer-visible response, it does count and stops the FR clock.

6 CSAT SURVEY-BASED
Customer satisfaction from post-resolution surveys. The most direct signal of how customers feel about our support.

Score Calculation

Average of all survey responses for resolved tickets.

CSAT = Average of CSAT Rating[0].average
WHERE CSAT Rating[0].count > 0

Scale: 1-5 (5 = very satisfied)

Response Rate

How many resolved tickets actually received a survey response.

Response Rate = Total Responses
/ Total Resolved Tickets x 100
Star distribution: The dashboard also shows how many customers gave 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5 stars. This helps distinguish between "lots of 3s" (mediocre) and "mix of 1s and 5s" (polarized).
CSAT is 4.1. What's the industry benchmark?
B2B SaaS support typically targets 4.3-4.5. Our 4.1 is reasonable but has room to improve. Research shows first-response speed correlates most strongly with CSAT — faster first responses generally lead to higher satisfaction scores.
Can we trust a 5% response rate?
It's directionally correct but not statistically robust. Respondents self-select: very happy and very unhappy customers are more likely to respond. The true average for all customers is likely closer to 3.5-4.0 (slightly lower than surveyed). To improve coverage, consider in-app prompts or shorter one-click surveys.

7 Who Resolves
Breakdown of who ultimately resolved each ticket — support team, engineering, or product.

Source Field

The Resolved By field (custom field tnt__resolved_by) on each ticket. Only three values are counted:

"Resolved by Support" — CX team solved it
"Resolved by Engineering" — needed code change/fix
"Resolved by Product" — needed product decision
Tickets without this field or with other values are excluded from this chart. This means the percentages represent only tickets where the CX Lead explicitly marked who resolved it.
86% resolved by support. Is that good?
Yes — it means most issues are handled without needing engineering escalation. The 8% engineering and 7% product indicate tickets that required code changes or product decisions. A healthy ratio is 80-90% support. If support drops below 70%, it might mean the product has too many bugs or the support team lacks access/tooling.
What about tickets resolved by the customer themselves?
Those typically go into "Resolved by Support" if the CX Lead guided the customer to the solution, or they might be closed without a "Resolved By" value if the customer stopped responding and the ticket timed out. The current field doesn't have a "Self-resolved" option.

Likely a cleanup sprint where the team dedicated time to clearing backlog. These spikes are positive — they show the team can burst when needed. The normal sustainable rate is ~35-40 resolved per week. Spikes above that indicate focused effort on reducing queue.

9 Severity Distribution
Donut chart of open tickets by severity level. Answers: "How critical is our current queue?"

Severity Levels

Four levels, set by the CX Lead based on customer impact:

Blocker — customer's core business is completely stopped
High — significant feature is broken, workaround exists
Medium — default, moderate impact on operations
Low — minor issue, cosmetic, or nice-to-have
83% Medium. Should we worry?
Medium is the default severity. Most tickets start as Medium unless the CX Lead explicitly escalates or de-escalates. The key metrics to watch are: Blocker count (should be near 0 — each one means a customer is down) and High count (should be declining week-over-week). The Medium majority is normal.
Who decides severity?
The CX Lead (tnt__assignee) sets severity during initial triage. Customers can request a severity level, but the CX Lead makes the final call based on actual business impact. Blockers should be verified — a "Blocker" that isn't actually stopping operations should be downgraded.

10 Aging Distribution
Bar chart showing how old open tickets are. Answers: "How much of our queue is stale?"

Simple calendar-day age of each open ticket.

Age = Today - Created date (in calendar days)

1-3dRecent — should have at least a first response by now
3-7dGetting stale — most should be resolved or in Awaiting Customer Response

62 tickets are 30+ days old. Why?
Most are in "Awaiting Development" or "Awaiting Product Assist" stages — waiting on engineering or product decisions, not support. Support has done their investigation but needs engineering to fix a bug or product to make a decision. Is aging measured in business days or calendar days?
Calendar days. A ticket created on Friday and checked on Monday is 3 days old, even though only 1 business day passed. This is intentional — the customer experiences calendar time, not business time. For SLA measurement, we use SLA-aware time; but for aging, calendar time is more honest about the customer wait.

11 Stage Dwell Analysis
How long tickets sit in each stage. Identifies bottlenecks — if tickets pile up in a stage, that's where the process is stuck.

What the table shows

For each stage: how many tickets are currently there, how long they've been there on average, the oldest one, and which accounts have the most tickets in that stage.

Count = tickets currently in this stage
Avg Age = mean(Today - Created date) for tickets in stage
Oldest = max(Today - Created date) for tickets in stage
Top Accounts = 3 accounts with most tickets in stage

Stage Definitions

  • QueuedNew, unactioned. Ticket just came in and nobody has picked it up yet. SLA clock is running.
  • WIP (Work In Progress)CX Lead is actively investigating. This is the "doing work" stage. SLA clock running.
  • Awaiting Customer ResponseWe asked the customer a question and are waiting for their reply. SLA clock is paused.
  • Awaiting DevelopmentSupport investigation is done; needs engineering to fix a bug or build a feature. SLA clock is running (this is a common SLA miss cause).
  • Awaiting Product AssistNeeds product team decision — feature scoping, priority call, or design input.
  • In DevelopmentEngineering is actively working on a fix. SLA clock running.
  • Reassigned to Customer SupportEngineering bounced it back to support — either the fix is deployed and needs verification, or it wasn't an engineering issue.
  • ReopenPreviously resolved, but customer reopened because the problem recurred or the fix didn't work. SLA clock restarts.
Most tickets are in "Awaiting Development." Is that a problem?
It means support has done their job (investigated, identified root cause) but engineering hasn't fixed it yet. This is an engineering capacity/priority issue, not a support issue. The actionable step is to work with engineering leads to prioritize these tickets in their sprint planning.
SLA clock runs during "Awaiting Development" — is that fair?
The current SLA policy only pauses during "Awaiting Customer Response." All other stages count against SLA. This means tickets stuck in engineering queues will miss SLA, which accurately reflects that the customer is still waiting. It's by design — it creates pressure to fix bugs faster.

15 Account Health Score COMPOSITE
A single 0-100 score that combines SLA performance, ticket aging, and customer sentiment into one risk indicator.

Formula

Weighted combination of three independent signals. Each component is normalized to 0-100.

Health Score = SLA% x 0.4
             + Inverse Age Score x 0.3
             + Sentiment Score x 0.3
40%
SLA Performance
Average of FR% and RT%
30%
Age Score
100 - min(Avg Age x 2, 100)
30%
Sentiment
100 - (Frustrated% x 3 + Unhappy% x 1.5), capped 0-100

SLA% Component

Average of the account's FR% and RT%. If an account has 80% FR and 60% RT, the SLA component is 70.

SLA% = (FR% + RT%) / 2

Inverse Age Score

Penalizes older tickets. An account with avg age 5 days scores 90. An account with avg age 50+ days scores 0.

Age Score = 100 - min(Avg Age x 2, 100)

0 days = 100 (perfect)
25 days = 50
50+ days = 0

Sentiment Score

Penalizes frustrated and unhappy sentiment more heavily. Based on DevRev's sentiment.label field (Happy/Neutral/Unhappy/Frustrated).

Sentiment Score = 100 - (Frustrated% x 3 + Unhappy% x 1.5)
Capped at 0 (floor) and 100 (ceiling)

Example: 10% Frustrated, 20% Unhappy
= 100 - (10x3 + 20x1.5) = 100 - 60 = 40

Health Badges

Healthy Score > 80
Watch Score 60-80
At Risk Score < 60
Aramex is red. What does that mean?
It's a composite of three independent signals: low SLA performance (e.g., 12% FR), high average ticket age, and frustrated customer sentiment. Three different data sources all pointing to account risk. This isn't one bad metric — it's a pattern across multiple dimensions.
Can the weights be changed?
Yes — the 40/30/30 split is configured in the dashboard code. If leadership wants to weight SLA more heavily (e.g., 60/20/20), that's a code change. The current weights were chosen to balance "are we meeting targets" (SLA) with "how long are customers waiting" (age) and "how do customers feel" (sentiment).
An account with 2 tickets shows "At Risk" — is that meaningful?
Low-volume accounts can get extreme scores. Two tickets both missing SLA = 0% SLA. The score is mathematically correct but statistically noisy. Focus health scores on accounts with 5+ tickets in the range. Accounts with very few tickets should be reviewed manually rather than algorithmically.

How it works

Targets are set in the dashboard sidebar — they can be changed to match what leadership expects. The gap is calculated automatically.

Gap = Target - Actual

Status:

Who sets the targets?

"Why don't your numbers match DevRev?" Different filter scope. We show Support-subtype only, excluding WMS and Roadmap. DevRev shows all ticket types.
"What's the SLA target?" Default SLA policy sla-28, same for all accounts. Schedule: Mon-Fri 10AM-8PM IST.
"Why is TAT so low compared to customer complaints?" TAT is SLA-aware — it excludes customer wait time and off-hours. Customers experience wall-clock time which includes nights, weekends, and their own response time.
"Is Friday AI saving time?" Yes. 4.2 min AI vs ~2.4h human P50 for root cause analysis. ~34x faster, ~900 hours saved per month at current coverage.
"Why are 62 tickets 30+ days old?" Most are in Awaiting Development or Awaiting Product stages — engineering/product backlog, not support delay. Support has investigated; waiting on fixes.
"Who's overloaded?" Check People tab, CX Lead Open Tickets table. Sort by Total column. Cross-reference with stage breakdown — high "Awaiting Dev" doesn't mean the person is slow.
"Which accounts are at risk?" Accounts tab, Health Score table. Red badges = at risk (score < 60). Composite of SLA misses, old tickets, and frustrated sentiment.
"Are we getting better?" Roadmap tab, Weekly Progress table. Green arrows = improving week-over-week. Compare current metrics vs targets to see remaining gap.
"Can we set per-account SLA targets?" Not currently. DevRev has one default policy. Per-account SLA requires configuring separate SLA policies — doable but needs DevRev admin work.
"Why is FR% lower than RT%?" FR clock never pauses — it runs from ticket creation to first response. RT clock pauses during "Awaiting Customer Response." FR is harder to meet, especially for overnight tickets.
"83% of tickets are Medium severity. Is that meaningful?" Medium is the default. Most tickets start as Medium unless explicitly escalated. Focus on Blocker (business stopped) and High (significant impact) counts instead.
"Resolve ratio is below 100%. When will we catch up?" At current rates, calculate: Open Backlog / (Weekly Resolved - Weekly Created) = weeks to clear. If Resolved < Created consistently, the backlog grows and we need either more capacity or fewer incoming tickets.
